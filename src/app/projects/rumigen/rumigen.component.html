Aim & Objectives

RUMIGEN - Towards improvement of ruminant breeding through genomic and epigenomic approaches; aims to deliver breeding programs with improved sustainability for small and local breeds in diverse environmental conditions, considering trade-offs between breeding objectives, preservation of genetic diversity and available technologies (including innovations developed in the course of the project). It will also lead to new precision farming management indexes that combine genomic, phenotypic and epigenetic information. This aim will be achieved through dedicated scientific activities and a multi-actors-driven approach, taking into account societal acceptance, feasibility, and overall resource and economic efficiency.

To deliver these new breeding programs, RUMIGEN will explore four levers.

Social acceptance of breeding tools

The social acceptance of new breeding techniques will be evaluated by creating a multi-actor dialogue by which a feedback loop is set up. A room of acceptance and a set of future scenarios will be developed, which will be tested in European participatory citizen consultations. The feedback will be used to refine the room of acceptance and scenario's to ultimately lead to a socially accepted plan for using new breeding techniques.

Quantitative genetics

Large scale existing data from commercial farms, including performances, pedigrees and genotypes, combined with meteorological records of an extensive range of breeds (Holstein, Montbéliarde, MRY), environments (France, Spain and the Netherlands), and traits (production, fitness) will be studied to investigate the trade-offs of adaptation and heat stress. Furthermore, genomic methods will be developed to describe local breeds' genetic diversity and enable their genetic improvement whilst maintaining diversity. The impacts of genomic selection on local breeds will be assessed, and the feedback will be implemented.

Genome Editing

Acceptable goals for genome editing in breeding programs will be defined based on dialogues with European breeders. The impact and efficiency of traditional and new breeding techniques will be compared, and the potential use of GE for welfare-related biodiversity purposes will be explored.

Epigenetics

The epigenetic lever aims to determine the impact of environmental stressors (metabolic demands during gestation, heat, immunity challenge and pathogens) on the epigenetic profiles of dairy cattle. The genetic and environmental components of epigenetic effects will be disentangled to find the genetic switches that will turn good genes ON and bad genes OFF.

Why

The ambition of the European Commission is to make Europe the worlds first climate-neutral continent by 2050. For this purpose, the EU Commission is currently drafting the roadmap for a "Farm to Fork" strategy, which aims for a fair, healthy, and environmentally friendly food system.

By providing efficient, sustainable, and socially acceptable breeding goals and programs, innovative genomic prediction methods and precision management tools, the RUMIGEN project will contribute to the Farm-to-Fork strategy.

The overriding concept behind RUMIGEN is that developing sustainable breeding in cattle, with animal health and welfare at the heart of it, will require a paradigm shift regarding the way that breeding goals are defined, and technologies are implemented in breeding schemes, as well as new knowledge and innovations.

Impact

The RUMIGEN project will have long-lasting impacts on the breeding sector, farmers, consumers, and society.

Project structure and partners

RUMIGEN is designed as a multi-actor approach that combines the expertise of 18 different partners that range from research institutes, management and dissemination partners to commercial companies. It represents a large part of the European Union. It has been established to encompass all the complementary skills and competencies required to maximize the reaching of RUMIGEN's ambitious objectives.
